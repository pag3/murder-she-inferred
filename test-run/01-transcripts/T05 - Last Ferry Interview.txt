"Everybody loved him," says Isla Voss. "Well... everybody who wasn't about to be exposed." Marcus Reed laughs, then stops. "That's a nasty way to put it, isn't it?" He insists he only argued with the columnist about a bad review. Paula Kent asks, "If Marcus is innocent, why did he hide the revised brochure?" Marcus answers, "Because it looked terrible, not because I killed anyone." Isla says she spent the whole crossing calming seasick passengers; two deckhands agree, though one remembers she disappeared for "maybe thirty seconds... maybe a minute." Jessica rewinds the recorder and hears the victim say, "No, no, listen to me. If Paula knew about the false bookings, she'd walk." Paula fires back, "Wouldn't you, if your name was on the paperwork?" Then she adds, quieter, "I was in the galley from 8:40 until the horn sounded. Ask Chef Benoit. Ask anyone." Chef Benoit does ask, and confirms it. He also says Paula returned carrying a cup she never served. Marcus keeps repeating, "I told him to wait. I told him to wait," which sounds damning until Jessica learns he meant the printer proof, not the ferry departure. The real break comes when Isla says the lounge clock stopped at 8:52, yet Jessica notices the battery cover was unscrewed after the storm, meaning the clock cannot prove anything at all. A passenger remembers hearing Paula and the victim argue early, but the ship's purser places Paula on the lower deck when the fatal dose was poured. Jessica finally turns to Isla. "You wanted that interview stopped, didn't you?" Isla answers, "Wanted? Perhaps. Needed? That's another matter." Her umbrella tip matches the puncture in the poisoned thermos seal, and the fake sympathy in her first statement collapses the moment Jessica mentions the hidden booking ledger. The sea is rough enough that every testimony arrives a little blurred by nausea and noise. A deckhand says Marcus spent half the crossing outside smoking, but another swears he saw him in the brochure office correcting departure notices. Jessica checks the printed proofs and finds Marcus really did mark a time change, though he hid it because the company owner had forbidden any mention of delays. Paula explains that the columnist planned to publish a piece on the ferry company's false booking guarantees. That would have embarrassed her because her signature appears on reservation confirmations, but it also would have ruined Isla's expansion deal with the island resorts. Isla dismisses that as gossip until Jessica discovers resort contracts folded inside the victim's notebook cover. [Foghorn] The purser remembers Isla insisting the interview lounge stay unlocked after dinner, even though it was normally closed during rough weather. Isla says that was for the columnist's convenience. Chef Benoit says it also kept his route to the galley exposed, making Paula easy to blame. Jessica asks who knew the victim favored tea from the lounge thermos. Everyone did. That is the problem with public habits: they invite private sabotage. Marcus says the victim threatened to print old bankruptcy rumors unless the ferry company bought advertising in his magazine. He sounds bitter enough to be dangerous, yet Jessica notices his hands shake whenever the ship lurches. A man in that condition would struggle to carry a brimming thermos through the corridor unnoticed. Isla, by contrast, moves with the practiced balance of someone who has crossed in storms for years. Paula reveals another detail she had hidden out of pride: she once trusted Isla with the reservation ledgers when the office was understaffed. If false bookings were buried in the records, Isla had opportunity to rearrange them long before the columnist came aboard. Jessica interviews seasick passengers wrapped in blankets along the saloon benches. One remembers hearing the victim say, "No one sells this route without my paragraph on the fraud." Another remembers a woman answering, "Then perhaps your paragraph never arrives." They cannot identify the voice. The ferry horn drowns too much out. [Wave Crash] Jessica examines the poisoned thermos seal and realizes the puncture is narrow, deliberate, and made after the tea was poured. Chef Benoit says none of his kitchen tools fit. The purser says the lounge umbrella stand was moved from its usual corner after the storm. Isla says she leaned on it while helping a passenger. Jessica stores that away. The company's owner, reached by ship radio, admits the false booking scheme had been generating cash for months. Marcus knew enough to suspect it. Paula knew enough to resent it. Isla knew enough to profit from it. The columnist knew enough to make everyone panic. Still, panic is not proof. Jessica reviews the recorder again and hears glassware rattling behind the argument, followed by the ferry horn and then a soft metallic tap. An umbrella tip against a thermos lid would sound exactly like that. Marcus becomes briefly persuasive as a suspect when Jessica discovers he shredded a memo from the owner. Yet the memo only warned him to stop correcting ticket counts in writing. He hid it because he feared losing his job, not because it tied him to murder. Paula looks stronger as a suspect when a passenger recalls seeing her carry a cup up from the galley. She admits it. She took hot water to the columnist because she hoped he would delay publication until the company corrected the bookings. Jessica believes her because Chef Benoit, two porters, and the engine clerk all place her downstairs again before the poisoning window closes. Isla remains the only person whose movements are consistently small enough to escape notice and important enough to matter. [Rain on Windows] Jessica inspects Isla's umbrella and finds the ferrule polished clean except for a trace of chemical residue near the tip. Isla says that proves nothing; the umbrella touched half the boat in the storm. The ship doctor says otherwise. The residue matches the sedative compound found in the thermos. Jessica then asks why Isla lied about the stopped lounge clock. Isla answers too quickly: because she thought it would help. Help whom? Isla does not say. The answer comes from the booking ledger hidden behind a panel in the brochure cabinet. Isla had been selling premium reservations twice, pocketing the quiet difference, and relying on canceled passengers to absorb the outrage. The columnist discovered the pattern and planned to name names, including hers. Marcus knew enough to be frightened, Paula knew enough to be compromised, but Isla knew enough to lose everything. When Jessica lays out the timeline, even the ship's roll seems to pause. Isla slipped into the lounge under the pretense of consoling the victim, used the umbrella tip to pierce the thermos seal, and then seeded the scene with just enough misleading details to send suspicion toward Marcus's temper and Paula's paperwork. The stopped clock was meant to pin the window to a moment when Paula looked vulnerable. The hidden ledger was meant to stay hidden until the ship docked and the records could be "lost." Instead, the storm delayed everyone just long enough for Jessica to compare all the small lies against the route of the ferry itself. Isla says she only wanted more time. Jessica replies that murder is what people call time purchased with another person's life. By the time the ferry reaches harbor, the passengers have retold the story three different ways. Some insist Marcus nearly exploded. Some say Paula never should have signed those forms. Some pity Isla because she looked so composed while the handcuffs went on. Jessica, staring at the wet deck under the dock lights, thinks the voyage captured the whole architecture of suspicion in miniature: motive scattered everywhere, opportunity disguised by motion, and truth waiting in the one person who seems calm enough to narrate everyone else's guilt.
