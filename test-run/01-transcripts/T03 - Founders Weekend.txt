Jessica arrives at the historical society hall just after treasurer Arthur Bell is found slumped beside a locked display case. Deputy Mara Keene says the case contains a deed that could ruin several land deals and embarrass half the town council. Felix Dunn, who chaired the festival, admits he argued with Arthur over missing donor money but claims he spent the fatal hour rehearsing on the bandstand with half the parade committee. Vivian Shore, the society president, says Arthur threatened to reveal that her family title to the inn was incomplete. She also insists she never entered the archive room, although her scarf pin turns up beneath the clerk's desk. Owen Pritchard says he drove Arthur home at ten, only to learn later that Arthur returned to the hall because someone called about the old deed. A waitress at the Wharf House remembers Owen ordering coffee there at 10:20, which keeps him away from the hall for part of the window, but not all of it. Jessica interviews the costume volunteers and learns Felix borrowed a master key ring for "decorations," then returned it with one key missing. [Band Music Continues] The archivist says Arthur planned to expose a fake donation ledger, and the forged entries would have embarrassed Vivian as well as Felix. [Crowd Cheering] Jessica finds that Vivian's scarf pin was bent weeks earlier during a quilting demonstration, which weakens the neat accusation against her. She also learns Owen's phone booth call to Arthur came from a gas station two towns over, timed after the body was already discovered, so Owen may be reckless but he is not the killer. When the marching band restages its route, Jessica realizes the bass drum from Felix's rehearsal hid the sound of the display case closing. She traces powdered plaster from the bandstand steps to Felix's shoes and finds the missing key taped inside his rolled festival banner. Felix breaks and admits Arthur caught him siphoning restoration funds; he lured Arthur back to the hall under the pretense of checking the deed, then staged the locked-case mystery to frame Vivian. Even so, Jessica notes that Vivian's lies about the title, Mara's impatience with Arthur, and Owen's desperate debts all made them plausible suspects until the timetable and key finally aligned. The mayor insists the crisis will not cancel the reenactment, which only gives Jessica more opportunities to observe people under pressure. Mara interviews the parade marshal, who says Felix left rehearsal twice and blamed a loose drum harness. The harness itself is dusty with plaster, as though it had been set on the archive floor. Vivian claims Arthur was destroying her family, yet the draft letters in her purse show she had already begun negotiating a settlement with him. Owen swears he never wanted Arthur dead, only quiet, because Arthur kept threatening to expose his gambling notes to the bank. Jessica studies the donor ledgers and finds that several suspicious entries were timed to coincide with every major restoration project Felix supervised. She also discovers that Arthur made margin notes beside each false donation, marking the initials F.D. in the same red pencil found snapped near the display case. [Marching Drums Resume] Children in tricorn hats rush through the square while Jessica questions the blacksmith who repaired the hall lock two weeks earlier. He remembers Vivian demanding a duplicate key for the wine cellar, not the archives, and he remembers Felix hanging around long enough to watch the key impressions being filed. At the inn, a housekeeper confirms Vivian spent part of the evening arguing with lawyers over property maps rather than plotting in the hall. Jessica next interviews the baker sponsoring the pie contest, who says Owen arrived flustered and smelling of gasoline, begging for change to make an urgent call. The baker thought little of it until Jessica matches the time to Arthur's mysterious summons back to the hall. That still does not make Owen the killer, but it proves someone used his desperation as camouflage. Felix, meanwhile, keeps insisting Arthur stumbled against the display on his own, despite the fact that the broken latch could only be reached from behind the cabinet. Jessica notices Felix never says "I found him"; he always says "he was there already," as though rehearsing a line he hopes will survive scrutiny. Vivian recalls overhearing Arthur say, "If the committee sees the real figures, Dunn is finished." Mara says that sounds like motive enough, yet motive is cheap in Cabot Cove when reputations and money start traveling together. [Fife Music] Jessica walks the route from the bandstand to the archives three times and times it against the parade order. Only someone who knew exactly when the cannon salute would fire could move unseen while everyone in the square looked toward the harbor. Felix helped schedule the salute himself. The historical society clerk remembers lending Felix a spool of blue ribbon just before the fatal window. That same ribbon turns up inside the rolled festival banner with the missing key. Owen says Felix asked him to deliver a note to Arthur, but Owen panicked and threw it away after the murder because he thought it made him look guilty. Jessica reconstructs the note from scraps in the trash basket behind the diner and reads just enough to see Arthur was promised "proof about the deed and the committee books." Vivian, confronted with that, finally admits she lied about the title because she feared Arthur would use it to force her resignation, but she denies killing him and Jessica believes her. Later that night Jessica reviews photographs from the Founders Day picnic and spots Felix wearing white plaster on his cuffs long before the hall crisis began, suggesting he had been tampering with the display mount in advance. She returns to the archives with Mara and discovers a hollow space behind the exhibit panel where the forged donor receipts had been hidden. Arthur must have found them and refused to be bought off. Felix tried to seize the records, Arthur resisted, and the struggle ended beside the locked case. By dawn, Jessica can explain the whole chain: Felix staged committee delays, diverted restoration funds, framed Vivian with the scarf pin, counted on Owen's debts to make him look desperate, and trusted the parade noise to bury the crucial sounds. When Mara arrests him on the green, Felix still protests that the festival would have collapsed without him. Jessica replies that institutions survive honesty far better than they survive charming thieves. [Crowd Murmurs] Even then the town gossip does not settle. A councilman accuses Vivian of hiding tax maps, a volunteer claims Owen sold replica muskets out the back door, and someone whispers that Mara pushed Arthur too hard during an audit. Jessica writes it all down, noting how quickly a civic celebration becomes a suspect carousel once pride, debt, and inheritance enter the picture. The reenactment finally proceeds under gray skies. The cannon fires, the drumline marches, and the bystanders cheer on cue, but Jessica keeps watching the faces instead of the costumes. She wants to understand not only who killed Arthur Bell, but why so many people were ready to believe the worst of one another before any evidence was sorted. That, she reflects, is what makes these small-town mysteries so dangerous: in a place built on shared history, every secret already knows the route home.
